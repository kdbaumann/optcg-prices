Key grading insights for these 20 cards: PSA 10: Typically adds 100–180% over raw for manga rares and anniversary SPs. The OP-13 Red SAAs are the strongest confirmed example ($7,950 raw → $24,100 PSA 10 = +203%). OP-01 Luffy Leader shows a lower premium (+84%) because it's a non-foil parallel rather than a true manga rare.

BGS 10 (Pristine): Typically 40–70% above PSA 10 for the same card in One Piece TCG. Requires 9.5+ subgrades on all four criteria. Estimated at 200–320% over raw for top-tier cards. Very few EN BGS 10s exist for most of these cards as of Apr 2026 — pop counts are near zero.

BGS Black Label (10/10/10/10): Only a handful of confirmed BL sales exist in all of One Piece TCG. The $45,000 Roger OP-09 BL is the highest confirmed sale but is considered a one-off outlier. PRB-01 Luffy Gear 5 BL sold for $10,601 (Nov 2025) — the most reliable BL data point. For most cards here, BL is theoretical. Do not factor BL into buy decisions without confirmed comparable sales.

📋 TOURNAMENT TYPES — How the OPTCG Competitive Ecosystem Works
🏅 CHAMPIONSHIP (CS)
The main annual competitive season. Runs in two stages:
Stage 1 – Regionals: Open tournaments at stores and large venues. Top finishers qualify for Finals. Prize cards distributed to Top 8, Top 16, Top 64.
Stage 2 – CS Finals: The national/world-level championship. Finalists receive exclusive trophy cards — some serialized or foil-stamped. The rarest prize cards in the game come from CS Finals.

Season naming: CS 22-23, CS 23-24, CS 24-25, CS 25-26.
Regions: Japan, North America, Europe, Latin America, Asia-Pacific, Oceania — each with separate regional finals.
Prize cards are: Foil-stamped alternates of existing cards OR entirely new artwork exclusive to the event.
💎 TREASURE CUP
Premier English-market large event series. Held multiple times per year in North America, Oceania, and occasionally Europe.
Prize structure:
 • All participants: Event Pack (4 promo cards)
 • Top 64: Exclusive foil-stamped promo card
 • Top 16: Upgraded promo (same card, different stamp)
 • Top 8: The most valuable Treasure Cup card

Key insight: Top 8 Treasure Cup cards have some of the lowest English print runs of any obtainable promo — typically ~256 copies globally per event wave. The 2023 Zoro (ST01-013) PSA 10 hit $21,600. Treasure Cup promos appreciate strongly over time.
⚔️ FLAGSHIP BATTLE
Store-level competitive events. Primarily a Japanese and Asia-Pacific format, though some EN events run it. More frequent than Treasure Cups — typically monthly or bi-monthly at local game stores.
Prize structure:
 • Top 8: "Top 8 Souvenir" promo (lower value)
 • Winner: "Winner's Memento" — foil-stamped promo card exclusive to 1st place

Value note: Individual Flagship Winner promos are lower value than Treasure Cup due to higher total print runs (many local stores run events simultaneously). Usually $50–$300 range. Exception: early 2023 winners like Zoro ST01 are worth $500+.
🎯 STANDARD BATTLE
Weekly/recurring store-level tournaments. Japan and Asia-Pacific focused. The most accessible entry point into organized play.
Prize structure:
 • All participants: 1 of 4 Standard Battle Pack cards (rotates by season)
 • Winner: Parallel Art promo of the featured card

Value note: Participation promos have almost no secondary market value ($1–$5). Winner parallels can reach $50–$300+ for popular characters. The Gear 5 Luffy Standard Battle winner promo reached ¥10,000 (≈$50) JP due to playability.
🎲 3-ON-3 / 8 PACKS BATTLE
Team and casual formats. 3-on-3 Cup is a 3-player team tournament; 8 Packs Battle is a sealed format where players draft 8 packs.
Historical significance: The Zoro "3-on-3 Cup Participant" promo (ST01-013 variant) is distinct from the Treasure Cup version — worth $600–$1,200. Many collectors confuse these two.
8 Packs Battle winner promos are newer (2024–2025) — Sanji ST10-004 winner is a current JP favorite at ¥10,000. (≈$50)
🔢 SERIAL NUMBER / PRE-RELEASE
The rarest category in the game. Serial Number cards have a unique print number stamped on them (e.g., "001/500"). Only distributed at the earliest tournaments before the game launched publicly.
Super Pre-Release 2022: Luffy ST01-001 Leader with a serial number — the first serialized prize card in OPTCG history. Fewer than 500 exist. $5,000–$6,000+ depending on serial number. Low serial numbers (#001, #002) command significant premiums.
Super Pre-Release Winner stamp: ST01-001 with "WINNER" stamp — separate from serialized version. ~$1,595 raw.
🌍 WORLD CHAMPIONSHIP
The pinnacle of OPTCG competition. Annual event featuring top players from all regions. Trophy cards awarded to finalists are the highest-prestige cards in the game.
CS Finals trophy cards (1st, 2nd, 3rd place) typically have print runs under 10 copies. The CS 23-24 Finals X. Drake (OP01-114) is the current grail from this tier.
Kaido CS 2023 Finals 2nd place (PSA 10): $17,998 — among the most expensive OPTCG cards ever sold.
Rebecca CS 2024 Top Prize (BGS 10): $17,000 confirmed.
🇯🇵 JAPAN-EXCLUSIVE FORMATS
Several formats are JP-only or Asia-dominant.
Grand Asia Open: Large regional event for Asia-Pacific. Prize cards often reach $200–$800 EN equivalent.
ONE PIECE DAY: Annual Bandai convention event. Exclusive promo cards distributed to attendees — not prize cards but convention exclusives. Often very low print runs.
Weekly Shonen Jump promos: Cards inserted into Japan's WSJ magazine. High initial print run but strong demand — the Luffy P-043 (treasure chest art) spiked to ¥10,000+ (≈$50) in late 2025 despite originally being a magazine supplement.
🇺🇸 EN vs 🇯🇵 JP — Key Differences in Prize Cards
🇺🇸 English Prize Cards
• Treasure Cup is the primary high-value event. Runs ~2–3× per year in NA.
• Championship Regionals run Oct–Dec each season with Top 8/16/64 prizes.
• EN prize cards often have lower print runs than JP because fewer EN tournaments run simultaneously — making Top 8 EN Treasure Cup cards scarcer than equivalent JP cards.
• Cards feature English card text, "EN" stamp or regional marker.
• EN promos are indexed on TCGPlayer and eBay Sold Listings — easier to price.
• Serial number cards exist only from the 2022 pre-release era.
🇯🇵 Japanese Prize Cards
• More tournament formats run in JP: Flagship, Standard Battle, 8 Packs, Grand Asia Open, plus all Championship tiers.
• JP Flagship winner cards typically have higher total copies than EN equivalents (more stores, more events).
• JP Championship Finals trophy cards are the rarest — sub-10 copies for 1st place trophies.
• JP promos are harder to price — Mercari Japan, CardRush, and Snkrdunk are the main sources.
• "Foil stamp" variants in JP often look identical to base except for the event logo — easy to counterfeit. Always buy PSA-graded for JP promos over $500.
⚠️ Collector's warning: EN and JP prize cards are different cards even when they share the same card number. An EN Championship Sakazuki (OP02-099) is not the same product as a JP Sakazuki promo — artwork may match but the stamp, language, and rarity designation differ. Always verify language and event when buying promos.

🔍 COLLECTOR TIPS — How to Buy Prize Cards Safely
🔐 Authentication Prize cards are among the most counterfeited in OPTCG. For any promo over $200, buy only PSA-graded copies. The event logo stamps on promos are often the first thing fakers get wrong — the font and metallic finish differ subtly. Use a 60× loupe to check the rosette dot pattern and the foil stamp quality.
🔄 Version Confusion Multiple versions of the same card exist for the same event. Zoro ST01-013 appears as: Treasure Cup Top 8, 3-on-3 Cup Participant, and Flagship Battle versions — each worth very different amounts. Always verify the event stamp logo and the placement tier before paying. A "Treasure Cup" stamp and a "Flagship" stamp on ST01-013 differ by $2,000+.
📈 Value Drivers Prize card value is driven by: (1) total copies in existence, (2) character popularity, (3) competitive playability, (4) event prestige. The 2023 Admiral trio (Sakazuki, Kuzan, Kizaru) are historically significant as the first EN Championship promos and have low copy counts. Early Treasure Cup promos (2023) will likely never re-enter supply — the only way to get one is to buy it.
📊 Pricing Sources EN promos: eBay Sold Listings + PriceCharting. JP promos: Snkrdunk App (most reliable), CardRush, Mercari Japan. Always filter by sold listings not active ones. Thin-market promos (sub-50 copies) often have stale or no pricing data — use the most recent sale as your anchor, not the listed price.
⚠️ Promo cards are never reprinted by Bandai. Once an event season ends, that prize card is gone forever from new supply. This structural scarcity is why early Treasure Cup and Championship promos have appreciated so dramatically — the OP01-era promos now have 2+ years of zero new supply entering the market. New prize cards are issued for current seasons but never retroactively reprint old ones. This page will be updated as new tournament seasons announce their prize card lineups.

The hidden gems at the bottom of the rarity ladder · Apr 2026

Why UC and C cards have almost no value — and when they do: Standard UC and C cards sell for $0.01–$0.50 because every box contains 45 Commons and 30 Uncommons — supply massively outpaces demand. However, three factors can push a UC or C above $1: (1) Competitive relevance — staple cards run as 4-ofs in every top deck command a small premium. (2) Foil/parallel treatment — even a C can be worth $5–20 if it has a parallel finish that's harder to pull. (3) Character popularity — Nami, Robin, Hancock parallels at any rarity command a premium from collectors. The takeaway: individual raw UC/C cards are essentially bulk. The play, if any, is in grading the rarest parallel foil versions of the most popular characters.

🎓 LOW-END GRADING ANALYSIS

Is It Worth Grading UC, C, R, and SR Cards?

PSA/BGS economics for non-chase cards · Apr 2026
$35–$75PSA grading cost all-in
3× ruleMinimum to break even
The math that kills most low-end grading plays: PSA standard tier costs approximately $35–$50/card all-in (submission fee + return shipping + insurance). BGS runs $60–$100+. That means a card needs to be worth at least $105–$150 graded just to break even at 3× raw value after platform fees (~13%). For any card worth less than $35 raw, grading is almost always a money-losing proposition — even a PSA 10 won't rescue it.

Key takeaways for low-end grading:

The 3× rule is non-negotiable. If a card won't sell for at least 3× its raw price in PSA 10, the math doesn't work after grading fees (~$35–$75) and platform fees (~13%). At standard PSA tier, your break-even PSA 10 sale price is roughly $120–$175 depending on shipping. That means only cards worth $40+ raw even deserve consideration.

PSA 9 is a disaster at low values. One Piece cards grade 9 frequently due to Bandai's back-edge whitening issue. A PSA 9 on a $50 raw card might sell for $60–$80 — a net loss after fees. This risk is why the 3× threshold is conservative, not aggressive.

The real low-end grading play: Bulk economy-tier submissions exist (some graders offer $12–$20/card in bulk lots of 100+). At those prices, OP-01 SR parallels of popular characters ($80–$200 raw) become genuinely interesting — a $12 submission on a $100 card that grades PSA 10 at $300+ is a 2.5× return net. Watch for PSA bulk submission windows and the occasional SGC economy tier.

Common and Uncommon base cards: Never grade. Ever. The math cannot work. Sell them in bulk lots of 100–500 cards on eBay for $10–$30 total rather than submitting individually. The only exception is if you have a verifiably pristine copy of a card with documented scarcity (e.g. an OP-01 print run error card) — and even then, authenticate first.
